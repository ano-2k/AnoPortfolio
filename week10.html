Week 10 - Telephone Etiquette

Session Details

Resource Person: Gowrisha Karuneswaran

Designation: Lecturer, Northern Uni

Topic: Telephone Etiquette

Answering and Greeting Callers

  • Answer calls within 2–3 rings to demonstrate professionalism and attentiveness to callers’ needs.
  • Use a courteous greeting, such as “Thank you for calling [department], how may I help you?” to set a positive tone.
  • Avoid unnecessary phrases like “good morning” to prevent errors; instead, rely on a warm tone for friendliness.
  • Speak directly into the mouthpiece or headset, ensuring clarity without distractions like eating or chewing gum.

Managing Calls

  • Ask permission before placing a caller on hold, e.g., “May I put you on hold?” and wait for their response.
  • Check back every 30–45 seconds when a caller is on hold, offering options to continue holding or receive a callback.
  • When transferring a call, explain the reason, verify the recipient’s availability, and provide the caller with the transferee’s details.
  • Never place the receiver on the desk without using the hold button to prevent callers from hearing office conversations.

Screening and Messaging

  • Avoid screening calls unless necessary; use neutral responses like “May I tell them who’s calling?” to maintain good relations.
  • When screening is required, use polite phrases like “She’s away from her office; may I take your name and number?” to avoid defensiveness.
  • Record messages accurately using forms, including caller’s name, number, date, time, message, and action needed (e.g., “Please call back”).
  • Deliver messages promptly and maintain confidentiality by folding or securing them to protect sensitive information.

Outbound Calls

  • Prepare for outbound calls by verifying the number, planning objectives, and anticipating questions or objections.
  • State your purpose clearly at the start of the call to ensure the conversation remains focused and efficient.
  • Take notes during the call to track key points and specify follow-up actions, such as when you’ll reconnect.
  • When leaving a voicemail, include your name, department, number, call time, message, and availability for a callback.

Professional Language

  • Use polite phrases like “Will you please?” instead of demanding ones like “You have to” to convey respect.
  • Refer to issues as “your concern” or “this situation” rather than “your problem” to maintain a positive tone.
  • Avoid abrupt phrases like “Hang on” or “I can’t help you”; instead, offer solutions, e.g., “I’ll transfer you to someone who can assist.”
  • Conclude calls professionally with past-tense phrases like “I’m glad we resolved this” and a clear “Good-bye,” avoiding slang.
